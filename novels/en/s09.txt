After parting ways with Cricket and Koza, the three of them headed toward an inn said to be near the entrance of the village.

When they had said their goodbyes, Cricket had urged them to get something to eat and spend the night at the inn.

“I’d love to have dinner with you, deshi,” she had said, “but if I get too friendly with certain adventurers, I’ll get scolded later. Still, come visit the guild anytime. You’ll always be welcome, deshi.”

“But please—no more pain,” Elts had replied.

Now, as they walked, Elts found himself recalling the exchange between Cricket and Koza.

Soon they stopped in front of a thatched hut with two cone-shaped roofs jutting out like the humps of a camel.

“Is this it?” Rins asked. “This must be the place Cricket mentioned.”

“Yeah, I’m pretty sure,” Swift said, pointing at a sign. “It says INN.”

Certain now, Elts stepped inside.

The first thing that caught their eyes was a wooden counter. Standing behind it was an elderly woman with white hair and a gentle smile.

Her warm expression immediately put them at ease.

“Um… we’d like to stay the night,” Swift said politely. “Do you have any rooms available?”

“Rooms?” the old woman chuckled softly. “Of course I do. Though truth be told, there’s never been a time when they weren’t available.”

The three of them exchanged glances.
Did that mean the place wasn’t very busy?

“You’re rookies, aren’t you?” the woman asked.

“Huh? Oh—yes,” Swift replied. “We arrived today. We just finished the beginner training at the guild.”

“I see, I see,” the woman said with a smile as she produced a guest ledger. “That girl must have put you through quite a bit.”

It was easy for the three of them to guess that “that girl” referred to Cricket.

“Please sign your names on this tablet with the click marker.”

“By signature, you mean our player names, right?” Swift asked. “Is it okay if I write first as our representative?”

“Yes, that’s fine,” she replied kindly. “But each of you must write your own name.”

Following Swift, Rins signed the ledger, and finally Elts wrote his name as well.

The old woman closed the book and gestured toward the hallway behind her.

“Take your time and make yourselves comfortable. Each of you will have your own room. You’re free to come and go as you like. Check-out is at ten tomorrow morning—that’s the only rule.”

“Understood,” Swift replied politely.

Then he turned to Elts and Rins.

“Hey, before we settle in, how about we take a quick look at our rooms and then go get something to eat?”

“Sounds good,” Rins said. “Come to think of it, we haven’t eaten anything since this morning.”

The idea of hunger suddenly struck Elts as strange.

“…Why do we even get hungry?”

“Well, because we haven’t eaten,” Swift answered matter-of-factly.

Elts realized how silly his question must have sounded.

“Yeah, I know, but… everything happening here is basically taking place inside our brains, right?”

Swift nodded thoughtfully.

“True. Our real bodies are in cryogenic storage, and we don’t need bathrooms either. Technically, eating shouldn’t even be necessary in this world. And yet…”

He shrugged.

“…we’re still hungry.”

Indeed, the three of them were undeniably hungry. That was an undeniable fact.

Elts continued, unable to hide a note of excitement.

“And another thing—don’t you think the idea of us staying at an inn here is kind of amazing? If we’re staying overnight, that means we’re actually living in this world. Cricket said time flows twenty-four times faster here than in reality.”

He looked at them, eyes bright.

“Isn’t that incredible?”

“…Sorry,” Swift said with a wry smile. “I’m not quite following.”

Elts summarized his thoughts.

“What I mean is—we’re eating, sleeping, living here. Completely. That means this place is another reality… a second world that exists alongside the real one.”

His speech sounded almost like a declaration.

Swift and Rins exchanged glances—and then chuckled.

“Elts… I think I get what you’re trying to say.”

They might have thought he was a strange guy.

But not a bad one.

Even in the short time they’d known each other, that much had become clear.

Realizing they were blocking the entrance with their conversation, the three bowed apologetically to the old woman and stepped into the hallway beyond.

Inside, the corridor was dimly lit and stretched straight ahead.

Elts walked along it, wondering where such a long passage could possibly fit inside that small hut.

“Alright then—let’s meet outside in about fifteen minutes,” he said.

There was no reply.

“…Huh?”

Elts turned around.

Swift and Rins were gone.

“…Did they go back outside?”

Just moments ago, he had clearly sensed them walking behind him.

Tilting his head in confusion, Elts reached for the door at the end of the corridor.

Inside was a small one-room space.

In one corner stood a bed. Beside it was a small round table with a stool. On the wall above the table was a small window.

Through it, Elts could see the guild perched atop the gentle slope, and in the foreground the goddess statue surrounded by flowers.

“Wow… that’s a nice view.”

He lay down on the bed.

For a moment, thoughts of the real world crossed his mind—but he quickly pushed them away.

After all, escaping that world was the very reason he had come here.

“…No point thinking about unnecessary things.”

He gazed at the guild tower glowing in the crimson sunset.

“For now… I’ll just focus on enjoying this world.”

Elts whispered the words quietly to himself.
